# gLLove

Your data is already out there. Companies collect it, model you with it, and use it to sell you things. gLLove asks a different question: what if you used your own data to understand yourself?

---

## The Problem

Every app on your phone knows something about you. Instagram knows what you linger on. Spotify knows your mood by what you play at 2am. Your bank knows your spending patterns. Your sleep tracker knows when you're restless. Research has shown that phone interaction data (how you swipe, how fast you type, when you pick up your phone) can predict your emotional state more accurately than your own self-report.

These companies use that signal to optimize engagement. To push better ads. To keep you scrolling.

None of them use it to help you.

They don't talk to each other directly, but they don't need to. Data brokers, ad networks, and hardware-level fingerprinting connect the dots across platforms. The way your specific monitor renders an ad can be used to identify you across websites. Your data isn't siloed. It's bought, sold, and correlated constantly. But none of that correlation ever flows back to you. Your sleep data doesn't inform your spending decisions. Your screen time doesn't talk to your calendar. Every app has a narrow slice of you, optimized for its own goals.

---

## The Idea

Centralize your own data. Pull it together from the sources that already have it (exports, APIs, local logs) and feed it into a system that works for you.

With enough data in one place, you can surface patterns that no single app would ever show you:

- A stressful event triggered a depressive episode that turned into a purchasing spree. You see the causal chain across mood, calendar, and bank data, not just a credit card statement.
- You sleep worst in winter, and that's also when you end up on the computer late at night. The sleep tracker alone can't tell you that, but sleep + screen time + seasonal data together can.
- Your productivity drops every time you skip the gym for more than three days. Exercise data meets task completion data.

These aren't hypothetical. They're the kind of inferences that become obvious once you stop looking at your life through one app at a time.

---

## Beyond Dashboards

The real play isn't just data visualization. It's using an LLM that actually knows you.

Current AI assistants are generic. They don't know your patterns, your triggers, your tendencies. But if you gave one access to your centralized personal data, it could be meaningfully personalized. Not in the shallow "here's your name" way, but in a way that reflects how you actually operate.

An LLM that knows your data could notice things you can't. Not because it's smarter, but because it doesn't forget, it doesn't rationalize, and it can hold your entire history in context at once.

---

## Philosophy

gLLove isn't about surveillance. It's about reclaiming data that's already being collected and using it for the person it actually belongs to.

The privacy argument against personal data collection assumes the alternative is that nobody has your data. That ship sailed. The real question is: given that this data exists, who benefits from it? Right now, the answer is platforms and advertisers. gLLove shifts that answer to you.
